3The session, replayed

Eleven moments. Each one: what was said, what the AI did, what shipped. Steering is the job — the first prompt matters less than the corrections.

12:19 · The briefing

Mark described the client and the goal in one long, casual voice-note-style message: a friend's exam-prep business, review it honestly, research the competition, propose improvements, showcase the AI tools used, deploy as a shareable web page — by the afternoon. He also set the working style: study consulting approaches first, clarify before executing, be a trusted mentor, "no participation trophies." Full text in section 4.

12:20–12:55 · Recon, clarifying questions, plan — and an adversarial review of the plan

Three AI agents explored in parallel (the client's website; internal references [internal — redacted]). Then the AI asked Mark four scoping questions (language? publish where? how ambitious visually? hard deadline?) — answers locked the contract. A plan was drafted, stress-tested by a planning agent (it caught a 30-minute budget overrun and a privacy leak risk in the repo layout), and then Mark ordered a second AI (Codex) to attack the plan: 13 findings, including "add a backup publishing path" and "students in screenshots may be minors — protect their names." The plan changed before any work started.

12:58–13:05 · Deploy first, build second

The repository went up and a placeholder page went live at minute seven. Reason: if publishing was going to fail, better to learn it at 13:05 with hours to react than at 14:55. From this moment the deliverable always existed; work only made it more complete.

13:01–13:14 · The research wave

Three research agents ran at once: a site auditor (browser screenshots, accessibility scan, technical checks), a market researcher (competitor prices from their own sites only — no aggregators), and a blueprint designer (the from-scratch architecture). The auditor's first claim — "the free-test page is dead" — was downgraded by the evidence rule to "alive but frozen in last season," which became the report's headline finding. The AI's error, caught by process, made the finding better.

13:19 · Two corrections and a handoff

Mark, about to leave with no internet: dropped one planned deliverable (a separate talking-points file — "no, keep it in the summary"), killed a stylistic direction left over from an earlier engagement ([internal — redacted] — "no personal-letter style, that was stupid"), and asked whether the session could finish alone. It could: everything pushes to the public web, so the laptop only needed power and a connection.

13:19–15:43 · The autonomous stretch

With Mark traveling, the session wrote the full report, built the Czech hero mockup (and screenshotted it to check its own work before embedding), ran a second Codex review of the finished page (12 findings — including a real self-contradiction between two sections and idioms too hard for B1 English readers), folded every finding, and pushed. Version 1 went live at 15:36. Honest note: the AI had promised "ten more minutes" at 13:19. It took 2 hours 17 — agent time estimates are narrative, wall clocks are real. (Our internal log first recorded these times wrong, too; the git history corrected it.)

15:54 · "I'm back" — and three hours of human time

Mark returned, read the live report, met with the client side, and thought. The session idled. This matters for honest accounting: roughly 40% of the 8-hour wall clock was the human reading, deciding, and living life — not the AI working. A solo founder replicating this should budget for their own attention, not just the AI's speed.

19:06 · The big steering message — version 2 is born

One long message changed the report's center of gravity: fix the unit-less "97/100", gloss the jargon ("hero" means nothing to non-developers), and — the real shift — "I don't want him to get a fish. I want to teach him how to fish." The how-to half of the report had ignored AI entirely; Mark wanted it to dominate with it. He also set guardrails: no curriculum ("AI is about learning while doing, not syllabuses"), no tool rankings, $20/month is enough, build model-agnostic skills. Full (lightly redacted) text in section 4.

19:06–19:52 · Research before writing, again

Two research agents studied how people actually learn AI (a "couch to 5K for AI" program, a teaching skill by a well-known educator, Anthropic's free fluency course, practitioner habits), and a planning agent stress-tested the design — its sharpest catch: the draft "value table" was exactly the fish-handing the new section was supposed to replace. Cut. Mark rejected the plan once more with comments (first half of the report stays; the learning layer is "assisted self-paced learning — your business is the course"), then approved.

19:52–20:08 · Build + triple review

Section 13, the kitchen-reveal in section 12, twelve "With AI" recipe lines in the roadmap, renumbering, the mockup fix — then three review lanes at once: Codex round 3 (10 findings — mostly "$20 plan can do everything" stated too strongly; softened), Gemini as a UX researcher (5 findings — jargon glosses, navigation aids; 4 folded, 1 honestly deferred), and an accessibility scan (clean). Live and verified by 20:20.

20:29 · "What we're missing is a trace"

Mark's last steering: make the session itself visible — simplified replay, honest numbers including idle time, and the full prompts behind one deliberate click. You are reading the result.

4The actual prompts

The replicable artifacts, full length. Mark's messages are quoted as typed (casual, imperfect — that's the realism). The agent prompts were written by the orchestrator AI; they are the most reusable part: swap the business details and they work for any small company.

4.1 · Mark's briefing (12:19) — the prompt that started everything

Mark said (abridged only where marked)

"I have a new project-client: my friend is running a little website in a small town in the Czech Republic. He is a 19-year-old just finishing his high school, and he started this little business of preparing students in his local town for the test exams for the high schools. […] He shared with me a website, and I want to review it, try to understand the business, try to understand the competition, and think about suggestions for improvement. I definitely have suggestions for improvement on the UX and UI side […] give it some consolidated design style guide, and basically elevate the whole interface experience without being super flashy or anything that's not necessary. We also need to be appropriate and truly honestly representing him himself and his business […] What I want from you is to start in some logical, structured fashion. Help me think through how we set up a plan. I think at first we should research some consulting concepts, small business consulting or literally startup consulting concepts […] I'm going to be meeting him in a few hours in the afternoon, and I want to have a little brief. […] We want to be encouraging. Remember, it's a very young entrepreneur starting his business, still a student at high school. We don't want to be just giving participation trophies either. We want to be like a trusted mentor for him […] Alongside, we're also going to document what kind of steps we have taken, what kind of tools we have used, and why. […] Maybe the very best start would be to start with a Q&A session […] to just clarify what we're trying to do and why, and what's in scope and out of scope. […] The output should be a little report that we will upload in HTML form […] deploy it on a Github pages so that we can very easily share it." [references to internal tools and an earlier engagement removed]

Notice what makes this brief work despite the casual style: who the client is, the honest-mentor tone contract, the deliverable format, the deadline, and permission to structure the approach. Content beats polish.

4.2 · The four scoping questions the AI asked back (12:20)
  • Language? → English, simple (B1–B2), Czech terms in brackets — "Ondra and Šimon speak English and need to practice it."
  • Publish when/where? → Public link before the meeting, neutral repo name, search engines told not to index, ~30-day takedown.
  • Visual ambition? → Everything: audit + style guide + hero mockup + a ground-up "design it from scratch" blueprint.
  • Hard deadline? → Link in hand by ~15:00.

Four questions, four binding answers. This ten-minute exchange prevented hours of building the wrong thing — it was the highest-leverage step of the day.

4.3 · The "voice card" — pasted into every research agent's instructions
VOICE CARD (binding): simple English (B1-B2), short sentences. Czech terms in brackets with correct diacritics on first use. Tag every claim [Observed] (verified today, with URL + time) or [Inferred] (reasoned, say from what). Every number/fact needs a receipt: URL + short quote + as-of timestamp. First names only (Ondra, Šimon) — never surnames.

This is a context harness in five lines — the same idea as the "business file" the review recommends in its section 13. Write the rules once, paste them everywhere, and every helper produces compatible work.

4.4 · Research agent A1 — the site auditor (dispatched 12:58, full prompt)
You are A1, the site-audit probe for a same-day consulting review of https://prijimackynostress.cz/ — a Czech website where two 19-year-old students (Ondra, Šimon) sell mock entrance-exam sessions (přijímačky nanečisto) and tutoring (doučování) for 9th-graders in Dobříš, CZ. Your findings feed a published report, so evidence discipline is absolute. HARD DEADLINE: return within 35 minutes. If incomplete, return partials explicitly tagged NOT VERIFIED rather than miss the window. Your final message is structured data for a synthesizer, not prose for a human. [VOICE CARD — see 4.3] ## Task 1 — FIRST ACTION: /testzdarma verification (evidence-level rule) Earlier recon got a 404 on …/testzdarma/ — possibly a broken primary lead-gen funnel, possibly a fetch artifact. Verdict "CONFIRMED BROKEN" requires ALL of: (a) the actual nav link href from the homepage HTML traced to its real target, (b) direct URL fetch of that target, (c) www and non-www variants, (d) with and without trailing slash, (e) checked at desktop AND mobile viewport, each with HTTP status codes recorded + timestamps. Anything less → verdict "FRAGILE FUNNEL" (works but no monitoring / unclear path — describe exactly what you saw). Record the precise evidence chain — this is the report's potential headline finding. ## Task 2 — Screenshots (≤8 files total) [tooling chain: real browser first, escalate after ONE failed attempt, never retry-loop] Hygiene (binding): fresh browser context (no cookies); dismiss cookie banners before shooting; note any student names visible in testimonials (likely minors — flag them so the synthesizer can exclude before publication). ## Task 3 — Audit (grouped findings, ≤150 lines total output) Groups: SEO/discoverability · Technical · Copy/content · Trust signals · UX/navigation. For each finding: one-line what, one-line why-it-matters for THIS business (parents of 14-15-year-olds choosing exam prep), evidence tag + receipt, severity (P1 blocks customers / P2 friction / P3 polish). Check specifically: page titles + meta descriptions per page, H1 structure, schema.org presence, mobile rendering, page weight, CTA paths from homepage to purchase, pricing discoverability, testimonial anonymity, Gmail vs domain email, OG tags, 404 behavior, HTTPS, console errors. ## Output schema (exactly this structure) ### TESTZDARMA VERDICT … ### SCREENSHOT MANIFEST … ### FINDINGS … ### TOP 5 … ### NOT VERIFIED …

The two ideas worth stealing: the evidence-level rule (define what "confirmed" requires before the agent looks) and "partials tagged NOT VERIFIED beat missing the window."

4.5 · Research agent A2 — market & prices (full prompt, trimmed)
You are A2, the competitive+pricing probe … HARD DEADLINE: 35 minutes … PRICE SOURCE RULE (binding): a price may ONLY be cited from the competitor's OWN website/checkout fetched today — never from aggregators, articles, or memory. If not findable on their own site: write "price not public". Normalize: state price per session vs per course (with duration). Mark promotional prices as such. ## Task 1 — National competitor table (cap 8 rows) … ## Task 2 — Local landscape (HARD TIME-BOX: 10 minutes, then stop) …"LOCAL LANDSCAPE: thin/unmapped" is an acceptable honest answer — do not pad. ## Task 3 — Exam-market context: official exam dates from cermat.cz ONLY … ## Task 4 — Positioning gap (3-5 bullets) grounded in the table …

"Unmapped is an acceptable answer — do not pad" is the anti-hallucination sentence. Give every research agent permission to come back empty-handed, or it will invent.

4.6 · Research agent A3 — the ground-up blueprint (full prompt, trimmed)
You are A3, the ground-up blueprint designer … Your deliverable: the report's centerpiece — "if we designed this site from scratch, what is the simplest, most logical architecture" — optimized for SEO/marketing and customer acquisition, while staying simple enough for TWO STUDENTS to maintain. SIMPLICITY IS THE GRADED DELIVERABLE. BINDING FRAME — open with this and mean it: "No rebuild needed now." The blueprint is the target picture for when they outgrow the current site; the first moves are quick wins INSIDE WordPress/Elementor. A rebuild-first implication is a failure mode of this deliverable. Required sections: 1. NO REBUILD NEEDED NOW (≤6 lines) · 2. SITEMAP (≤8 pages, each: job-to-be-done | target Czech keyword | one CTA) · 3. MODULES & SEAMS (≤8 reusable blocks) · 4. ACQUISITION FUNNEL (+ the seasonal clock) · 5. PLATFORM STANCE (honest stay-vs-rebuild, "stay" is acceptable) · 6. THREE CZECH HERO HOOKS · 7. WHAT I'D MEASURE (≤5 lines)

Note the guardrails: hard caps on everything, an explicit failure mode named in advance, and "simplicity is the graded deliverable." Agents optimize what you grade.

4.7 · The adversarial review charters (Codex ×3, Gemini ×1)

Codex, plan review (12:35):

You are an adversarial technical reviewer. Review the plan below for implementation feasibility, untested assumptions, security risks, and dependency concerns. Find at least 3 serious problems. Also propose better approaches where you see them. … Judge feasibility of the pipeline mechanics (publishing, screenshot tooling, timing math, privacy/leak vectors, review gates, fallbacks), the realism of the schedule, and the soundness of the report content plan.

Codex, finished-report review (15:30, round 2):

You are an adversarial reviewer of a CLIENT-FACING consulting report … Find problems BEFORE the client does. Review charter, in priority order: 1. FACTUAL OVERCLAIMS: any claim stated more strongly than its evidence tag supports; any number without a source; any internal contradiction. 2. TONE FAILURES: anything condescending, harsh, mocking, or sycophantic. 3. SIMPLE-ENGLISH violations a Czech B1-B2 reader would stumble on. 4. CZECH LANGUAGE: wrong diacritics or grammar in the bracketed terms. 5. PRIVACY: any full surname of a private person. 6. STRUCTURE: broken internal references. Severity: P1 = must fix before the client sees it …

Gemini, "Chief UXR" review (20:02):

You are 'Chief UXR' — a UX-research reviewer … readers: two 19-year-old Czech founders with B1-B2 English + their parents/partners skimming. Your charter — UX research, not visual nitpicking: 1. AUDIENCE FIT: where would a B1 reader lose the thread or feel lectured? 2. READING FLOW at 15 sections: where does attention die? 3. COMPREHENSION TRAPS: jargon without gloss, tables that fail on a phone. Max 5 findings, each citing a concrete element. If you cannot name the element, drop the finding.

Three different reviewer personalities on purpose: feasibility attacker, claims attacker, reader advocate. Same-model review agrees with itself; different models and different charters catch different failures.

This is what "steering" means: not better prompts, but reading the output and reacting with judgment. The corrections carried more information than the briefing.

5Replicating this

You do not need the multi-agent setup to copy the method. The session above is four moves, and every one works in a plain ~$20 AI chat:

  1. Brief casually, but completely — who it's for, the tone contract, the deliverable, the deadline (4.1). Then let the AI ask you questions before it starts (4.2).
  2. Write the rules once — a voice card / business file pasted into every conversation (4.3).
  3. Demand evidence discipline — receipts with dates, "not verified" allowed, severity tags (4.4–4.6). Define what "confirmed" means before the AI looks.
  4. Get a second opinion from a different AI — paste the finished work into another tool with an attack charter (4.7). Then read it yourself and steer (4.8) — the human corrections were the highest-value tokens of the whole day.

That is the same loop the review's section 13 teaches — this page is just the loop, caught on camera.
