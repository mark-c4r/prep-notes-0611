3The session, replayed

The moments that mattered, in order — what was said, what the AI did, what shipped. (The engagement is closed; this replay is complete.) Steering is the job — the first prompt matters less than the corrections.

12:19 · The briefing

Mark described the client and the goal in one long, casual voice-note-style message: a friend's exam-prep business, review it honestly, research the competition, propose improvements, showcase the AI tools used, deploy as a shareable web page — by the afternoon. He also set the working style: study consulting approaches first, clarify before executing, be a trusted mentor, "no participation trophies." Full text in section 4.

12:20–12:55 · Recon, clarifying questions, plan — and an adversarial review of the plan

Three AI agents explored in parallel (the client's website; internal references [internal — redacted]). Then the AI asked Mark four scoping questions (language? publish where? how ambitious visually? hard deadline?) — answers locked the contract. A plan was drafted, stress-tested by a planning agent (it caught a 30-minute budget overrun and a privacy leak risk in the repo layout), and then Mark ordered a second AI (Codex) to attack the plan: 13 findings, including "add a backup publishing path" and "students in screenshots may be minors — protect their names." The plan changed before any work started.

12:58–13:05 · Deploy first, build second

The repository went up and a placeholder page went live at minute seven. Reason: if publishing was going to fail, better to learn it at 13:05 with hours to react than at 14:55. From this moment the deliverable always existed; work only made it more complete.

13:01–13:14 · The research wave

Three research agents ran at once: a site auditor (browser screenshots, accessibility scan, technical checks), a market researcher (competitor prices from their own sites only — no aggregators), and a blueprint designer (the from-scratch architecture). The auditor's first claim — "the free-test page is dead" — was downgraded by the evidence rule to "alive but frozen in last season," which became the report's headline finding. The AI's error, caught by process, made the finding better.

13:19 · Two corrections and a handoff

Mark, about to leave with no internet: dropped one planned deliverable (a separate talking-points file — "no, keep it in the summary"), killed a stylistic direction left over from an earlier engagement ([internal — redacted] — "no personal-letter style, that was stupid"), and asked whether the session could finish alone. It could: everything pushes to the public web, so the laptop only needed power and a connection.

13:19–15:43 · The autonomous stretch

With Mark traveling, the session wrote the full report, built the Czech hero mockup (and screenshotted it to check its own work before embedding), ran a second Codex review of the finished page (12 findings — including a real self-contradiction between two sections and idioms too hard for B1 English readers), folded every finding, and pushed. Version 1 went live at 15:36. Honest note: the AI had promised "ten more minutes" at 13:19. It took 2 hours 17 — agent time estimates are narrative, wall clocks are real. (Our internal log first recorded these times wrong, too; the git history corrected it.)

15:54 · "I'm back" — and three hours of human time

Mark returned, read the live report, met with the client side, and thought. The session idled. This matters for honest accounting: roughly half of the 9-hour wall clock was the human reading, deciding, and living life — not the AI working. A solo founder replicating this should budget for their own attention, not just the AI's speed.

19:06 · The big steering message — version 2 is born

One long message changed the report's main focus: fix the unit-less "97/100", explain the jargon ("hero" means nothing to non-developers), and — the real shift — "I don't want him to get a fish. I want to teach him how to fish." The how-to half of the report had ignored AI entirely; Mark wanted it to dominate with it. He also set guardrails: no curriculum ("AI is about learning while doing, not syllabuses"), no tool rankings, $20/month is enough, build model-agnostic skills. Full (lightly redacted) text in section 4.

19:06–19:52 · Research before writing, again

Two research agents studied how people actually learn AI (a "couch to 5K for AI" program, a teaching skill by a well-known educator, Anthropic's free fluency course, practitioner habits), and a planning agent stress-tested the design — its sharpest catch: the draft "value table" was exactly the giving-answers-instead-of-teaching the new section was supposed to replace. Cut. Mark rejected the plan once more with comments (first half of the report stays; the learning layer is "assisted self-paced learning — your business is the course"), then approved.

19:52–20:08 · Build + triple review

Section 13, the behind-the-scenes additions to section 12, twelve "With AI" recipe lines in the roadmap, renumbering, the mockup fix — then three review lanes at once: Codex round 3 (10 findings — mostly "$20 plan can do everything" stated too strongly; softened), Gemini as a UX researcher (5 findings — jargon glosses, navigation aids; 4 folded, 1 honestly deferred), and an accessibility scan (clean). Live and verified by 20:20.

20:29 · "What we're missing is a trace"

Mark's steering: make the session itself visible — simplified replay, honest numbers including idle time, and the real prompts behind one deliberate click. Before building it, the AI researched how practitioners publish session logs (the convention: narrative summary first, full material behind an explicit link, annotate the corrections — and we disclose idle vs. active time here, which most session reports don't). Live by 20:40.

20:47 · "We're not done yet" — a lesson in ordering work

Mark caught the AI listing the remaining steps in the wrong order — his read-through was queued before a research task that could still change the report's content, which would have made him read a stale version twice. The correction was bigger than the bug:

Mark said

"You need to think about the sequence of our steps as well, the structure of our steps. Take a step back and think: Research → thinking structures in layers → what's needed to be done, why, and when, and why are we actively ordering it […] Then go and start scoping and maybe shaping and planning how to do that, and only then do we start doing, and the very last step is the polishing edits."

The re-ordered close-out: open research first (it can change content) → fold + polish → the human read (once, on the frozen version) → close-out rituals → future reminders last. Steps ordered by what feeds what, not by the order they came to mind. This log was also declared a living document at this moment — appended until the engagement closes, including the making and correcting of the log itself.

20:55 · The crowd reports back (Grok), and the phone test passes

Mark ran the deep-research prompt manually on grok.com and pasted the dossier in. Useful for honesty: the crowd's conclusion — "not another course, but a progressive practice loop embedded in real business tasks, with persistent context, light accountability, built-in verification" — described section 13 as already written. Two additions made it in after link verification: the Elements of AI Czech course (elementsofai.cz, checked live) and a sixth beginner trap (changing tools too often; practitioners consistently report that 30 days deep on one tool beats novelty). The raw dossier was archived as untrusted data; named endorsements stayed out of the report because we could not confirm them with a second source. Mark also reported his mobile check: clean on Android, except a few milliseconds of font switching on first load — a standard web-font tradeoff we accept rather than over-engineer.

21:16 · Research round 2, then the stop decision

A follow-up research turn pressure-tested the report's four biggest guesses. Three got corroborated: parents really do find tutoring through Czech Facebook offer-and-demand groups; individual tutors who are visible online do publish their rates, which supports the report's advice to give tutoring a public price anchor; and the student-to-student positioning has a named, successful Czech precedent (Doučebnice.cz — verified against their own site before it entered the report). One stayed honestly thin (maps-vs-SEO), and two questions came back "no evidence exists yet" — which is an answer too. One claimed price figure failed verification (the source page returned 404), so it stayed out of the report entirely. Then the call: stop researching. A third round would chase data the crowd already said doesn't exist; the remaining unknowns are cheaper to answer in the real world — one post in a parents' group, two mystery-shop emails, five customer conversations. Knowing when research stops compounding is part of the method.

21:01 & 21:20 · The same mistake, twice — caught by process, not intention

Twice in twenty minutes, a private process note nearly landed in the public repository: a compound shell command changed its working folder mid-chain, and a later step wrote to the wrong place. Both times the verification habit caught it before anything was pushed — zero exposure. The embarrassing part is the second time: it happened minutes after the AI documented the first incident, and the failing command even contained a written reassurance ("correct folder this time") that the check proved false. Two rules entered the permanent log: verify state, never assert it — and use one mechanical fix (absolute paths for any cross-repository write) instead of trusting attention. An AI repeating a mistake it just wrote down is exactly why this whole setup runs on checks, not promises.

21:26–21:30 · The approval, and the session writes its own handover

Mark's last message of the day approved all changes and ordered the close-out: re-apply the research learnings across the whole report, update this log with the prompts and their TL;DRs, one last polish, then ship. The session's final act was a handoff note for the next session — live state, the remaining steps in dependency order (content-changing work first, the freeze last, exactly as the 20:47 lesson taught), and the lessons headed for the permanent log. A fresh session starts with zero memory; the handoff note is how the work survives.

23:07 → close · The close-out session, and one last correction

A fresh session read the handoff and ran the close: a consistency pass over the whole report with both research rounds in mind (eight small edits, including two stale counts in the review's own section 12), then this page's prompt appendix and the final numbers above. The final measurement caught two errors worth admitting. The token figures published earlier were overcounted — the corrected, smaller numbers are in section 2, with the method. And the timestamps on the last three moments above were originally written from narrative memory and ran up to an hour fast; git commit times corrected them to what you see now. That is the same lesson this page already taught itself once today (the 15:36 moment): narrative time drifts; measured time doesn't — and it happened again the very same evening, caught again by the same method. Then: freeze. What you are reading is the final version.

4The actual prompts

The replicable artifacts, full length. Mark's messages are quoted as typed (casual, imperfect — that's the realism). The agent prompts were written by the orchestrator AI; they are the most reusable part: swap the business details and they work for any small company. Each entry opens with a one-line TL;DR ("too long; didn't read" — the short version), so you can skim before you open anything.

4.1 · Mark's briefing (12:19) — the prompt that started everything

TL;DR: review a friend's exam-prep business honestly, study the competition, propose improvements, document the method, publish as a shareable page by mid-afternoon — in a trusted-mentor tone, no participation trophies.

Mark said (abridged only where marked)

"I have a new project-client: a young entrepreneur running a little website in a small town in the Czech Republic. He started this little business of preparing students in his local town for the test exams for the high schools. […] He shared with me a website, and I want to review it, try to understand the business, try to understand the competition, and think about suggestions for improvement. I definitely have suggestions for improvement on the UX and UI side […] give it some consolidated design style guide, and basically elevate the whole interface experience without being super flashy or anything that's not necessary. We also need to be appropriate and truly honestly representing him himself and his business […] What I want from you is to start in some logical, structured fashion. Help me think through how we set up a plan. I think at first we should research some consulting concepts, small business consulting or literally startup consulting concepts […] I'm going to be meeting him in a few hours in the afternoon, and I want to have a little brief. […] We want to be encouraging. Remember, it's a very young entrepreneur starting his business, still a student at high school. We don't want to be just giving participation trophies either. We want to be like a trusted mentor for him […] Alongside, we're also going to document what kind of steps we have taken, what kind of tools we have used, and why. […] Maybe the very best start would be to start with a Q&A session […] to just clarify what we're trying to do and why, and what's in scope and out of scope. […] The output should be a little report that we will upload in HTML form […] deploy it on a Github pages so that we can very easily share it." [references to internal tools and an earlier engagement removed]

Notice what makes this brief work despite the casual style: who the client is, the honest-mentor tone contract, the deliverable format, the deadline, and permission to structure the approach. Content beats polish.

4.2 · The four scoping questions the AI asked back (12:20)

TL;DR: before building anything, the AI asked four questions; the four binding answers — language, where to publish, ambition, deadline — became the contract for the day.

  • Language? → English, simple (B1–B2), Czech terms in brackets — "Ondra and Šimon speak English and need to practice it."
  • Publish when/where? → Public link before the meeting, neutral repo name, search engines told not to index, ~30-day takedown.
  • Visual ambition? → Everything: audit + style guide + hero mockup + a ground-up "design it from scratch" blueprint.
  • Hard deadline? → Link in hand by ~15:00.

Four questions, four binding answers. This ten-minute exchange prevented hours of building the wrong thing — it was the highest-leverage step of the day.

4.3 · The "voice card" — pasted into every research agent's instructions

TL;DR: five binding lines pasted into every helper agent — simple English, Czech terms in brackets, every claim tagged and receipted, first names only.

VOICE CARD (binding): simple English (B1-B2), short sentences. Czech terms in brackets with correct diacritics on first use. Tag every claim [Observed] (verified today, with URL + time) or [Inferred] (reasoned, say from what). Every number/fact needs a receipt: URL + short quote + as-of timestamp. First names only (Ondra, Šimon) — never surnames.

This is a context harness in five lines — the same idea as the "business file" the review recommends in its section 13. Write the rules once, paste them everywhere, and every helper produces compatible work.

4.4 · Research agent A1 — the site auditor (dispatched 12:58, full prompt)

TL;DR: audit the site under strict evidence rules — define what "confirmed broken" requires before looking, screenshots with privacy hygiene, grouped findings with severity; partial answers allowed, invention not.

You are A1, the site-audit probe for a same-day consulting review of https://prijimackynostress.cz/ — a Czech website where two 19-year-old students (Ondra, Šimon) sell mock entrance-exam sessions (přijímačky nanečisto) and tutoring (doučování) for 9th-graders in Dobříš, CZ. Your findings feed a published report, so evidence discipline is absolute. HARD DEADLINE: return within 35 minutes. If incomplete, return partials explicitly tagged NOT VERIFIED rather than miss the window. Your final message is structured data for a synthesizer, not prose for a human. [VOICE CARD — see 4.3] ## Task 1 — FIRST ACTION: /testzdarma verification (evidence-level rule) Earlier recon got a 404 on …/testzdarma/ — possibly a broken primary lead-gen funnel, possibly a fetch artifact. Verdict "CONFIRMED BROKEN" requires ALL of: (a) the actual nav link href from the homepage HTML traced to its real target, (b) direct URL fetch of that target, (c) www and non-www variants, (d) with and without trailing slash, (e) checked at desktop AND mobile viewport, each with HTTP status codes recorded + timestamps. Anything less → verdict "FRAGILE FUNNEL" (works but no monitoring / unclear path — describe exactly what you saw). Record the precise evidence chain — this is the report's potential headline finding. ## Task 2 — Screenshots (≤8 files total) [tooling chain: real browser first, escalate after ONE failed attempt, never retry-loop] Hygiene (binding): fresh browser context (no cookies); dismiss cookie banners before shooting; note any student names visible in testimonials (likely minors — flag them so the synthesizer can exclude before publication). ## Task 3 — Audit (grouped findings, ≤150 lines total output) Groups: SEO/discoverability · Technical · Copy/content · Trust signals · UX/navigation. For each finding: one-line what, one-line why-it-matters for THIS business (parents of 14-15-year-olds choosing exam prep), evidence tag + receipt, severity (P1 blocks customers / P2 friction / P3 polish). Check specifically: page titles + meta descriptions per page, H1 structure, schema.org presence, mobile rendering, page weight, CTA paths from homepage to purchase, pricing discoverability, testimonial anonymity, Gmail vs domain email, OG tags, 404 behavior, HTTPS, console errors. ## Output schema (exactly this structure) ### TESTZDARMA VERDICT … ### SCREENSHOT MANIFEST … ### FINDINGS … ### TOP 5 … ### NOT VERIFIED …

The two ideas worth stealing: the evidence-level rule (define what "confirmed" requires before the agent looks) and "partials tagged NOT VERIFIED beat missing the window."

4.5 · Research agent A2 — market & prices (full prompt, trimmed)

TL;DR: build the competitor price table from the companies' own websites only — "price not public" and "local landscape unmapped" are acceptable answers.

You are A2, the competitive+pricing probe … HARD DEADLINE: 35 minutes … PRICE SOURCE RULE (binding): a price may ONLY be cited from the competitor's OWN website/checkout fetched today — never from aggregators, articles, or memory. If not findable on their own site: write "price not public". Normalize: state price per session vs per course (with duration). Mark promotional prices as such. ## Task 1 — National competitor table (cap 8 rows) … ## Task 2 — Local landscape (HARD TIME-BOX: 10 minutes, then stop) …"LOCAL LANDSCAPE: thin/unmapped" is an acceptable honest answer — do not pad. ## Task 3 — Exam-market context: official exam dates from cermat.cz ONLY … ## Task 4 — Positioning gap (3-5 bullets) grounded in the table …

"Unmapped is an acceptable answer — do not pad" is the anti-hallucination sentence. Give every research agent permission to come back empty-handed, or it will invent.

4.6 · Research agent A3 — the ground-up blueprint (full prompt, trimmed)

TL;DR: design the from-scratch site architecture — but open with "no rebuild needed now", and simplicity is the graded deliverable.

You are A3, the ground-up blueprint designer … Your deliverable: the report's centerpiece — "if we designed this site from scratch, what is the simplest, most logical architecture" — optimized for SEO/marketing and customer acquisition, while staying simple enough for TWO STUDENTS to maintain. SIMPLICITY IS THE GRADED DELIVERABLE. BINDING FRAME — open with this and mean it: "No rebuild needed now." The blueprint is the target picture for when they outgrow the current site; the first moves are quick wins INSIDE WordPress/Elementor. A rebuild-first implication is a failure mode of this deliverable. Required sections: 1. NO REBUILD NEEDED NOW (≤6 lines) · 2. SITEMAP (≤8 pages, each: job-to-be-done | target Czech keyword | one CTA) · 3. MODULES & SEAMS (≤8 reusable blocks) · 4. ACQUISITION FUNNEL (+ the seasonal clock) · 5. PLATFORM STANCE (honest stay-vs-rebuild, "stay" is acceptable) · 6. THREE CZECH HERO HOOKS · 7. WHAT I'D MEASURE (≤5 lines)

Note the guardrails: hard caps on everything, an explicit failure mode named in advance, and "simplicity is the graded deliverable." Agents optimize what you grade.

4.7 · The adversarial review charters (Codex ×4, Gemini ×1)

TL;DR: three attack briefs — a feasibility attacker for the plan, a claims attacker for the finished report, a reader advocate for the experience — each ordered to find real problems, not to praise. The same claims charter ran a fourth time on this very page.

Codex, plan review (12:35):

You are an adversarial technical reviewer. Review the plan below for implementation feasibility, untested assumptions, security risks, and dependency concerns. Find at least 3 serious problems. Also propose better approaches where you see them. … Judge feasibility of the pipeline mechanics (publishing, screenshot tooling, timing math, privacy/leak vectors, review gates, fallbacks), the realism of the schedule, and the soundness of the report content plan.

Codex, finished-report review (15:30, round 2):

You are an adversarial reviewer of a CLIENT-FACING consulting report … Find problems BEFORE the client does. Review charter, in priority order: 1. FACTUAL OVERCLAIMS: any claim stated more strongly than its evidence tag supports; any number without a source; any internal contradiction. 2. TONE FAILURES: anything condescending, harsh, mocking, or sycophantic. 3. SIMPLE-ENGLISH violations a Czech B1-B2 reader would stumble on. 4. CZECH LANGUAGE: wrong diacritics or grammar in the bracketed terms. 5. PRIVACY: any full surname of a private person. 6. STRUCTURE: broken internal references. Severity: P1 = must fix before the client sees it …

Gemini, "Chief UXR" review (20:02):

You are 'Chief UXR' — a UX-research reviewer … readers: two 19-year-old Czech founders with B1-B2 English + their parents/partners skimming. Your charter — UX research, not visual nitpicking: 1. AUDIENCE FIT: where would a B1 reader lose the thread or feel lectured? 2. READING FLOW at 15 sections: where does attention die? 3. COMPREHENSION TRAPS: jargon without gloss, tables that fail on a phone. Max 5 findings, each citing a concrete element. If you cannot name the element, drop the finding.

Three different reviewer personalities on purpose: feasibility attacker, claims attacker, reader advocate. Same-model review agrees with itself; different models and different charters catch different failures.

4.9 · The Grok deep-research prompt, round 1 (run by Mark at grok.com; results pasted back 20:55)

I'm researching how people ACTUALLY teach non-technical young founders (teens/early 20s, small real businesses) to work effectively with AI assistants — not prompt-engineering courses, but learn-while-doing approaches where their own business is the practice ground. Please search X posts and linked blogs/threads from the last ~12 months and tell me: 1. What approaches, habits, and micro-programs do practitioners report actually STICK with beginners? (daily reps, context files, verification habits, demo-to-a-friend mechanics, "couch to 5k for AI"-style programs, anything else people swear by) 2. What do people report does NOT work? What does the X community say about Matt Pocock's /teach skill in the mattpocock/skills repo — real usage reports, what people praise, what they criticize, notable forks/remixes? 4. Worth stealing: numbered themes, "first-hand experience vs. opinion/hype", "how widespread + your confidence", and a 30-day evidence bar — the report's receipts discipline, pointed at a crowd. The automated Grok lane had failed on quota in the morning; this manual run is the "second lane" the review's section 12 talks about. Its conclusion independently matched section 13 as already written — the most reassuring kind of research result.

5Replicating this

You do not need the multi-agent setup to copy the method. The session above is five moves, and every one works in a plain ~$20 AI chat:

  1. Brief casually, but completely — who it's for, the tone contract, the deliverable, the deadline (4.1). Then let the AI ask you questions before it starts (4.2).
  2. Write the rules once — a voice card / business file pasted into every conversation (4.3).
  3. Demand evidence discipline — receipts with dates, "not verified" allowed, severity tags (4.4–4.6). Define what "confirmed" means before the AI looks.
  4. Get a second opinion from a different AI — paste the finished work into another tool with an attack charter (4.7). Then read it yourself and steer (4.8) — the human corrections were the highest-value tokens of the whole day.
  5. Send research to attack, not to confirm — when a tool can search the live web, hand it your own conclusions and ask for evidence against them (4.9–4.10), then verify anything you keep before it goes public.

That is the same loop the review's section 13 teaches — this page is just the loop, caught on camera.
